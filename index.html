Frequently Asked Questions

How do I use this calculator?+

1. Weigh your empty candle container (mould/jar).
2. Fill it with water to your desired fill-height.
3. Weigh it again. The difference is the water weight.
4. Enter this weight, select your wax, choose your fragrance load, and click calculate!

How does the calculator work?+

The calculator uses the weight of water a container can hold and the specific density of the selected wax to determine the total weight of wax and fragrance needed to fill that same volume.

What is 'Fragrance Load'?+

Fragrance Load (FO) is the percentage of fragrance oil relative to the weight of your wax. A 6-10% load is standard for most waxes.

What does 'Cure Time' mean?+

Curing is the time required for the wax and fragrance oil to bind together for the best scent throw. This can range from 3 days to 2 weeks depending on the wax.

Why is my candle top bumpy or rough?+

This is common with natural waxes like soy. It's often caused by the wax cooling too quickly. Try warming your jars before pouring or controlling the room temperature.

What is 'tunnelling'?+

Tunnelling is when the wick only melts a small diameter of wax in the center, leaving a ring of hard wax on the outside. It's usually caused by a wick that is too small for the container diameter.

What are 'wet spots'?+

These are patches where the wax has pulled away from the glass. It's a cosmetic issue caused by wax shrinkage during cooling and doesn't affect the burn.

At what temperature should I add fragrance oil?+

This depends on the wax, but a general rule is around 85°C (185°F). Adding it too hot can burn off the scent; too cold and it may not bind properly.

At what temperature should I pour my wax?+

Pouring temperature also varies by wax type. A cooler pour (around 55-60°C or 130-140°F) often results in smoother tops for soy wax.

What is a 'melt pool'?+

The melt pool is the layer of liquid wax that forms around the wick as the candle burns. For the first burn, you should let it reach the edges of the container to prevent tunnelling.

What's the difference between essential oils and fragrance oils?+

Fragrance oils are synthetic and specifically designed for high heat in candles. Essential oils are natural but can be volatile and may not produce a strong scent in a candle.

Why is my candle smoking?+

A smoking wick is often too long. Trim your wick to about 1/4 inch (6mm) before each burn. It can also be a sign of a draft or too much fragrance oil.

Can I use crayons to color my candles?+

It's not recommended. The pigments in crayons can clog the wick and create a fire hazard. Always use candle-specific dyes.

What is 'frosting' on soy wax?+

Frosting is the formation of tiny white crystals on the surface of natural waxes. It's a normal characteristic of soy wax and doesn't impact the candle's performance.

Do I have to use a double boiler?+

Yes, you should never melt wax directly on a heat source as it's flammable. A double boiler (or a pot inside a larger pot of water) provides gentle, even heat.

How do I choose the right wick?+

Wick selection depends on your wax type, container diameter, and fragrance/dye load. It often requires testing. Consult your supplier's wick guide as a starting point.

What is 'scent throw'?+

'Cold throw' is the scent when the candle is unlit. 'Hot throw' is the scent released when the candle is burning. Curing your candle properly is key to a good hot throw.

Why did my candle crack?+

Cracks are usually caused by the wax cooling too rapidly. Ensure your workspace is free from drafts and the surface you're cooling on isn't too cold.

Can I reuse old candle jars?+

Yes! Clean them thoroughly by freezing the remaining wax (it should pop out) or using a heat gun, then wash with hot, soapy water.

What's the best wax for beginners?+

Many beginners start with soy wax (like GW 464) because it's natural, cleans up easily with soap and water, and is generally forgiving.

Do I need to add any other additives?+

Most modern container waxes are pre-blended and don't require additives. Some waxes, like paraffin, can benefit from additives like Vybar for scent retention or Stearin for hardness.

Is candle making safe?+

It is safe if you follow precautions. Never leave melting wax unattended, keep a fire extinguisher nearby, and work in a well-ventilated area away from children and pets.
